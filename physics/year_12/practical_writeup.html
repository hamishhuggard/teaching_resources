1. Variables & Parameters

Independent Variable

Achieved The independent variable is the weight force ($F_g$) that provides tension on the string ($F_T$).

Merit This force was systematically varied from $1.5\text{ N}$ to $3.9\text{ N}$.

Achieved The standard unit of measurement is Newtons ($\text{N}$).

Merit This was achieved by adding $50\text{ g}$ slotted masses to a $50\text{ g}$ mass hanger.

Merit The total mass was converted to weight force using $F_g = mg$, where $g = 9.8\text{ N kg}^{-1}$.

Dependent Variable

Achieved The dependent variable is the time period ($T$) for one complete rotation of the rubber bung.

Merit This duration was accurately measured using a digital stopwatch, recording the total time for 10 full oscillations.

Minimum Value of Independent Variable

Merit The smallest weight force used was $1.5\text{ N}$, yielding a period of $6.6\text{ s}$ for 10 rotations.

Excellence At this lower limit, the bung began to sag significantly below the horizontal plane (at an angle of roughly $10^\circ$).

Excellence This vertical displacement compromised the accuracy of the horizontal radius ($r$) of rotation.

Excellence Reducing the weight any further would have introduced a systematic geometric error as the motion ceased to be horizontal.

Excellence Consequently, $1.5\text{ N}$ was chosen as the justifiable lower limit to maintain circular motion in a horizontal plane.

Maximum Value of Independent Variable

Merit The maximum weight force used was $3.9\text{ N}$, corresponding to a time period of $3.9\text{ s}$ for 10 rotations.

Merit The absolute uncertainty for this measurement is calculated from half the range of the raw data.

Excellence The calculated uncertainty is $\text{absolute uncertainty} = \frac{\text{Max} - \text{Min}}{2} = \frac{4.2 - 3.9}{2} = 0.15\text{ s}$.

Merit This value is bounded by typical human reaction time limits of approximately $0.1\text{ s}$ to $0.2\text{ s}$.

Excellence The percentage uncertainty for this shortest period is $\frac{0.15}{3.9} \times 100\% \approx 3.8\%$.

Excellence Any higher weight forces would make the period too fast to count accurately, setting a clear practical upper limit.

Controlled Variables

Merit We successfully controlled the radius of rotation ($r$) by attaching a fixed reference marker (alligator clip/blue tape) to the string.

Merit The speed of rotation was carefully modulated so this marker remained stationary just below the bottom of the glass tube.

Excellence According to the theoretical relationship $W = m_{\text{bung}} 4 \pi^2 r^2 T^{-2}$, $T$ is highly sensitive to variations in $r$.

Excellence If the radius slipped and increased by just $6\text{ cm}$, it would cause a $\frac{6}{55} = 10.9\%$ change in the radius measurement.

Excellence This slip would cause a mathematically significant $5.3\%$ increase in the calculated period.

Merit We also strictly controlled the mass of the rubber bung ($m_{\text{bung}}$) by using the exact same physical bung across all trials.

Excellence If the mass of the bung fluctuated, the centripetal force required would change, violating the one-to-one dependency between $W$ and $T$.

2. Sources of Error

Parallax Error

Merit To eliminate parallax error, I aligned the reference marker directly at eye level during rotation.

Merit This ensured that looking up or down at the marker did not cause a perceived shift in its position relative to the tube.

Excellence By eliminating this alignment shift, we kept the radius constant and avoided a systematic measurement error.

Reaction Time Error

Merit Human reaction time when starting and stopping a stopwatch introduces a random error of about $0.1\text{ s}$ to $0.2\text{ s}$.

Excellence If we only timed one single rotation at high speed ($T \approx 0.40\text{ s}$), a $0.1\text{ s}$ delay creates an unacceptable $25\%$ percentage error.

Merit To successfully mitigate this, we timed 10 consecutive rotations as a single continuous block.

Merit We then divided the total time by 10 to calculate the average time period for a single rotation.

Excellence This spread the reaction time error over 10 periods, reducing the percentage error to a negligible $\approx 2.5\%$.

Multiple Trials

Merit To minimize the impact of random human error, we repeated each mass setting for 3 independent runs.

Merit This gave us a highly robust set of multiple trials from which to spot outliers.

Merit The absolute uncertainty in our timing was calculated using the formula $\frac{\text{range}}{2}$ for each mass setting.

Excellence This calculated uncertainty closely matched the limits predicted by our manual reaction time.

Merit We calculated and used the mean (average) value of these trials as our final data points to increase overall reliability.

3. The Results

The Power Relationship

Achieved The theoretical equation governing this circular motion system is $W = m_{\text{bung}} 4 \pi^2 r^2 T^{-2}$.

Achieved From our non-linear curve fit of Weight vs. Period, we found the experimental power relationship $W = 0.653 T^{-1.95}$.

Merit The power value of $-1.95$ is extremely close to the theoretical integer power of $-2$.

Excellence This result confirms that $W$ is inversely proportional to the square of the period ($W \propto T^{-2}$), validating our physical model.

The Gradient Comparison

Achieved We linearized our data by plotting $W$ on the y-axis against $T^{-2}$ on the x-axis, yielding $W = 0.611 T^{-2} + 0.0839$.

Merit The experimental gradient ($m_{\text{exp}}$) from this linear regression equation is $0.611\text{ N s}^2$.

Achieved The theoretical gradient is defined as the constant coefficient of $T^{-2}$, which is $\text{gradient} = m_{\text{bung}} 4 \pi^2 r^2$.

Merit Substituting our values ($m_{\text{bung}} = 0.052\text{ kg}$, $r = 0.55\text{ m}$) yields a theoretical gradient of $0.621\text{ N s}^2$.

Excellence We compared these by calculating the accuracy ratio: $\frac{\text{experimental}}{\text{theoretical}} \times 100\% = \frac{0.611}{0.621} \times 100\% = 98.4\%$.

Excellence This means our experimental gradient is only $1.6\%$ off from the predicted theoretical value.

Excellence This exceptionally high level of accuracy indicates that our experimental model and control of variables were highly successful.

The y-intercept

Excellence According to theoretical physics, if there is no period ($T \to \infty$), there is no circular motion, so the y-intercept should be exactly $0\text{ N}$.

Achieved Our experimental equation yielded a very small, non-zero y-intercept of $c = 0.0839\text{ N}$.

Excellence This intercept represents only $5.6\%$ of our smallest independent variable value ($1.5\text{ N}$).

Excellence Because this value is so tiny, we can confidently attribute this non-zero intercept to minor random variations rather than an uncontrolled systematic error.
