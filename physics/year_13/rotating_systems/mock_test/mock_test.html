QUESTION 1: Rotational Motion (The Pottery Wheel)

A person making a clay pot spins up a heavy pottery wheel from rest. The wheel accelerates uniformly from rest ($\omega_i = 0\text{ rad s}^{-1}$) to a final angular velocity of $\omega_f = 10.0\text{ rad s}^{-1}$ over a time of $t = 4.00\text{ seconds}$.

(a) [Achieved] Use a rotational equation of motion to show that the angular acceleration ($\alpha$) of the wheel is $2.50\text{ rad s}^{-2}$.

(b) [Merit] The wheel has a rotational inertia of $I = 1.50\text{ kg m}^2$. A constant frictional torque of $0.50\text{ N m}$ opposes this spin. Calculate the total applied torque ($\tau$) needed to cause the angular acceleration in (a).

(c) [Excellence] The person removes the applied torque and allows the wheel to spin at a constant angular velocity. She then drops a heavy lump of wet clay onto the outer edge, which increases the total rotational inertia ($I$) of the system. Explain what happens to the angular velocity of the spinning wheel when the lump of clay sticks to it. Name the physics conservation law that explains this change.

QUESTION 3: Satellites in Orbit

A satellite orbits the Earth in a stable circular path.

(a) [Achieved] What force holds the satellite in circular orbit (not centripetal)?

(b) [Merit] The satellite orbits at a distance of $r = 7.00 \times 10^3\text{ km}$ ($7.00 \times 10^6\text{ m}$) from the center of the Earth. Using the mass of the Earth ($M_E$) from the reference table, and setting the gravitational force equal to the centripetal force, calculate the orbital speed ($v$) of the satellite as it orbits at this radius.

(c) [Excellence] A geostationary satellite must stay directly above the exact same spot on Earth's equator. Explain why it is impossible to have a geostationary satellite orbit directly over Auckland, New Zealand (which is south of the equator).

QUESTION 4: Vertical Circular Motion

A rollercoaster cart of mass $m = 200\text{ kg}$ goes around a vertical loop-the-loop track. The radius of the loop is $r = 2.55\text{ m}$.

(a) [Achieved] Calculate the minimum speed ($v$) the cart must have at the very top of the loop so it does not lose contact with the track.

(b) [Merit] As the cart travels down from the top of the loop to the bottom of the loop, its speed increases. Explain how conservation of energy explains this increase in speed.

(c) [Excellence] At the top of the loop, gravity helps pull the cart down into its circular path. At the bottom of the loop, the track must push upwards to fight gravity and keep the cart moving in a circle. Use these ideas to explain why a passenger feels weightless at the top of the loop, but feels very heavy at the bottom.

Assessment Marking Guide

For Teacher Use Only — Do Not Print for Students

QUESTION 1

  • (a) [Achieved]: Shows correct substitutions: $10.0 = 0 + \alpha(4.00) \implies \alpha = 10.0 / 4.00 = 2.50\text{ rad s}^{-2}$.
  • (b) [Merit]: Shows clear two-step calculation:
    Net torque: $\tau_{\text{net}} = I\alpha = 1.50 \times 2.50 = 3.75\text{ N m}$
    Applied torque: $\tau_{\text{applied}} = \tau_{\text{net}} + \tau_{\text{friction}} = 3.75 + 0.50 = 4.25\text{ N m}$. Allow correct unit $\text{N m}$ or $\text{kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-2}$.
  • (c) [Excellence]: Identifies Conservation of Angular Momentum. Explains that because the rotational inertia ($I$) increases, angular velocity ($\omega$) must decrease to keep total angular momentum ($L = I\omega$) constant.

QUESTION 2

  • (a) [Achieved]: Identifies Gravity (Weight) and Tension (Do not accept centripetal).
  • (b) [Merit]: Correctly resolves components to find $F_c$:
    Vertical balance: $F_T\cos\theta = F_g \implies F_T\cos(52.0^\circ) = mg = 0.150 \times 9.80 = 1.47\text{ N} \implies F_T = 2.39\text{ N}$
    Horizontal force: $F_c = F_T\sin(52.0^\circ) = 2.39 \times \sin(52.0^\circ) = 1.88\text{ N}$.
    Alternative shortcut: $F_c = mg\tan\theta = 1.47 \times \tan(52.0^\circ) = 1.47 \times 1.280 = 1.88\text{ N}$.
  • (c) [Excellence]: Shows clear speed calculation using $F_c$:
    Formula step: $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{F_c \cdot r}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.88 \times 0.500}{0.150}} = \sqrt{6.27} \approx 2.50\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
    Explains qualitative change: If speed ($v$) increases, the required centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) increases. Because the horizontal component of the tension force ($F_T\sin\theta = mg\tan\theta$) provides this centripetal force, the angle $\theta$ must increase to make $\tan\theta$ larger.

QUESTION 3

  • (a) [Achieved]: Identifies Gravity / Gravitational Force.
  • (b) [Merit]: Calculation using $M_E$: $v = \sqrt{\frac{G M_E}{r}} = \sqrt{\frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 5.97 \times 10^{24}}{7.00 \times 10^6}} = \sqrt{5.69 \times 10^7} \approx 7540\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
  • (c) [Excellence]: Explains that the center of any orbital plane must align with Earth's center of mass. An orbit centered over Auckland would require continuous lateral thrust because gravity pulls directly toward Earth's center.

QUESTION 4

  • (a) [Achieved]: Calculation: $v = \sqrt{2.55 \times 9.80} = \sqrt{25.0} = 5.00\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
  • (b) [Merit]: Explains conversion of Gravitational Potential Energy ($E_p = mgh$) at top to Kinetic Energy ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) at bottom, accelerating the cart.
  • (c) [Excellence]: Explains that "felt weight" is the normal support force ($F_N$). At the top, gravity provides centripetal force ($F_N \approx 0 \implies$ weightless). At the bottom, $F_s$ must overcome gravity and provide upward centripetal acceleration ($F_N = F_g + F_c \implies$ heavy).
